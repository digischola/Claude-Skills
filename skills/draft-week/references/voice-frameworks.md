# Voice Frameworks (Distilled from Top Writers)

Source: Perplexity research 2026-04-18. These are codifiable techniques from writers known for both quality and scale.

The skill should apply ONE primary framework per post, matched to the content type. Never mix frameworks mid-post (creates voice soup).

## 1. Dickie Bush — Structure and Scannability

**Core principle:** Write in layers. Simplify complex ideas.

**Key techniques:**
- **3x3 Method.** Every post boils down to 3 main points. Each point has 3 supporting beats. Forces removal of filler.
- **4A Framework (for idea generation).** Analyze a topic through 4 lenses: Actionable, Analytical, Aspirational, Anthropological.
- **Visual rhythm.** Short sentences. Ample whitespace. Line breaks every 1-2 sentences.

**Use when:** writing teaching / framework posts (Pillar 1 audits, Pillar 3 tactics).

**Applied example:**
> When I audit a landing page, I check 3 things.
>
> 1. The hook. Does it answer "why now, why me, why this"?
> 2. The proof. Is the testimonial above the fold or buried?
> 3. The friction. How many clicks to the CTA?
>
> Last week on a Thrive Retreat page:
> Hook: vague.
> Proof: slide 4.
> Friction: 6 clicks.
>
> Fixed all three. Conversion doubled in 72 hours.

## 2. Alex Hormozi — Value Equation

**Core principle:** Craft offers / posts so compelling refusal feels illogical.

**Key techniques:**
- **Value Equation.** Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice).
- **Friction Reduction.** Copy emphasizes reduction of time AND effort. Double-lever the denominator.
- **Trimming & Stacking.** List every friction the reader faces, explicitly name removing them.
- **Grand Slam Promise.** Make concrete, specific, measurable claims.

**Use when:** writing conversion-oriented posts, offers, case studies (Pillar 1, Pillar 3).

**Applied example:**
> I audited the landing page in 15 minutes.
> We cut the checkout from 4 steps to 1.
> Sales increased 188% in 48 hours.
>
> No new ad spend. No new traffic. Same audience. Shorter path.

## 3. David Perell — Intellectual Assets

**Core principle:** Every post is a long-term intellectual asset, not disposable content.

**Key techniques:**
- **Information Capture.** Systematically collect the best ideas you encounter.
- **Asset Creation.** Write for the long compounding effect, not the 24-hour feed.
- **The Bridge.** Writing is a bridge to connect with peers and clients, not self-promotion.

**Use when:** long-form pillar LinkedIn posts that could become evergreen-pinned content.

**Applied example mindset:** when writing a post, ask "will this still matter in a year?" If yes, invest in the polish. If no, keep it loose and ship fast.

## 4. Naval Ravikant — Compression

**Core principle:** High-signal, low-noise communication.

**Key techniques:**
- **Principle over jargon.** Explain underlying mechanics from first principles. Jargon hides knowledge; first principles share it.
- **Extreme distillation.** Edit ruthlessly. A thought that takes 30 words should become 10.
- **Leverage identification.** Write about strategic leverage (code, media, labor, capital) rather than time-for-money.

**Use when:** X single tweets, tight observations, manifesto-style posts.

**Applied example:**
> Instead of: "We optimize the omnichannel attribution funnel."
> Write: "We track exactly where your buyers come from."

## 5. Paul Graham — Plain English and Honesty

**Core principle:** Write simply so the reader expends energy on the idea, not the prose.

**Key techniques:**
- **Inclusivity.** Assume readers are intelligent but may not be native English speakers. Use ordinary words.
- **Aggressive cutting.** Edit until the prose is direct. If a word can be removed without losing meaning, remove it.
- **Truth over style.** Never decorate weak ideas with strong writing. Fix the idea first.

**Use when:** writing for cross-cultural audiences (AU/SG/NA), technical explanations, thought-leadership.

**Applied rules (Orwell's 6 + Graham):**
1. Never use a long word where a short one works.
2. If a word can be cut, cut it.
3. Never use passive where you can use active.
4. Never use jargon if an everyday equivalent exists.
5. Never use "Furthermore," "Moreover," or "In conclusion." These are AI tells.
6. Never use hype words: unlock, revolutionize, game-changer, in today's fast-paced world.

## 6. Cole Schafer — Conversational Copy

**Core principle:** Writing should feel like a conversation with a friend at a bar.

**Key techniques:**
- **Sticky Notes rule.** Keep the core idea small enough to fit on a sticky note before expanding.
- **Visceral language.** Use sensory words. "The Stripe notification hit at 11 PM" beats "I received a payment late at night."
- **Slippery Slide.** Every sentence's sole job is to pull the reader into the next sentence.

**Use when:** WhatsApp Status, casual Instagram captions, BTS posts.

## Sentence-level best practices (apply across all frameworks)

### Variance in sentence length (critical for anti-AI-detection)

Humans vary sentence length drastically. LLMs default to uniform lengths. To preserve human voice:

- A 15-word explanatory sentence must be followed by a 3-word punch.
- Never write 5 consecutive sentences of the same length.
- One-word sentences are allowed. Use sparingly. Like this.

### Opener verbs (active, energetic)

Replace weak passive structures with active, specific verbs.

| Passive / weak | Active / specific |
|---|---|
| "The conversion rate was improved by the new technology" | "The new technology accelerated the conversion rate" |
| "It was decided that..." | "I decided to..." |
| "A decision was made to..." | "I made the call to..." |

### Power connectors

Maintain momentum without sounding academic.

**Use:** "Here is why." "The reality is." "Consider this." "Which means." "So:"
**Avoid:** "Furthermore." "Moreover." "In conclusion." "Additionally." "On the other hand."

### Plain English rules (Orwell / Graham synthesis)

1. Never use a metaphor, simile, or figure of speech you see often in print.
2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
3. If a word can be cut, cut it.
4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
5. Never use a foreign phrase, scientific word, or jargon word if an everyday English word exists.
6. Break any of these rules sooner than write anything outright barbarous.

## Framework-to-content-type matching

| Content type | Primary framework | Why |
|---|---|---|
| LP audit / diagnosis post | Dickie Bush (3x3) | Scannable structure, parallel construction |
| Case study post | Hormozi (Value Equation) | Quantifies outcomes, reduces perceived effort |
| Thought leadership / manifesto | Perell + Naval | Long-lived asset + tight compression |
| Freelance-ops insight | Graham (Plain English) | Cross-cultural clarity |
| Vulnerable / personal story | Schafer (Conversational) | Friend-at-a-bar intimacy |
| X single tweet | Naval (Compression) | One insight, tight |
| X thread | Dickie Bush (3x3) + Naval (last tweet) | Structured arc, punchy close |
| Instagram caption | Schafer | Visual carries weight, caption invites |
| WhatsApp Status | Schafer | Lowest polish, most casual |

## AI-detection avoidance checklist

Before shipping, verify:

- [ ] No em dashes (universal rule)
- [ ] No "Furthermore," "Moreover," "In conclusion"
- [ ] No hype words ("unlock," "revolutionize," "game-changer," "in today's fast-paced world")
- [ ] No 5+ consecutive sentences of uniform length
- [ ] No overly balanced parallel constructions (3 sentences all starting with "The X is Y.")
- [ ] At least one unexpected word or phrase (something an LLM would not produce by default)
- [ ] At least one specific number, name, or date
- [ ] At least one small imperfection (casual aside, parenthetical, self-correction)

The `validate_post.py` script enforces a subset of these automatically. The rest is human judgment.
