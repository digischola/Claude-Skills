# CRO Fundamentals Checklist

Audit criteria for conversion rate optimization fundamentals. Each criterion has a description, what good looks like, common failures, and scoring guidance.

---

## 1. Headline Clarity (Weight: High)

**What to evaluate:** Can a visitor understand what this page offers within 3 seconds of landing?

**Good:**
- States the core value proposition in plain language
- Answers "what is this?" and "why should I care?" immediately
- Specific to the audience (not generic "welcome to our website")
- Matches the ad or traffic source that brought the visitor

**Common failures:**
- Clever/cute headlines that sacrifice clarity for creativity
- Company name as headline instead of value proposition
- Too long — headline becomes a paragraph
- Jargon or insider language the visitor doesn't know yet

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Crystal clear value prop, impossible to misunderstand. 7-8 = Clear but could be sharper. 5-6 = Takes a moment to understand. 3-4 = Confusing or generic. 1-2 = Headline is missing, irrelevant, or actively misleading.

---

## 2. CTA Placement & Copy (Weight: High)

**What to evaluate:** Is the primary call-to-action visible, compelling, and easy to act on?

**Good:**
- Primary CTA visible above the fold without scrolling (mobile AND desktop)
- CTA repeated at logical points down the page (after social proof, after features, at bottom)
- Button text is action-oriented and specific ("Book Your Free Consultation" not "Submit")
- CTA visually dominates — largest button, highest contrast color on page
- Only ONE primary CTA per page (secondary actions de-emphasized)

**Common failures:**
- CTA buried below the fold
- Generic "Submit" or "Learn More" button text
- Multiple competing CTAs with equal visual weight
- CTA color blends into the page background
- No CTA repetition — only one at the bottom
- CTA text doesn't match what happens next (expectation mismatch)

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Impossible to miss, specific action text, repeated perfectly. 7-8 = Visible and clear but minor placement or copy issues. 5-6 = Present but easy to overlook or generic. 3-4 = Hard to find or confusing. 1-2 = Missing or completely broken.

---

## 3. Form Friction (Weight: High — for lead gen / booking pages)

**What to evaluate:** How much effort does the form require? Every field is friction.

**Good:**
- Minimum viable fields (name + email/phone for lead gen, date + time + contact for booking)
- Labels are clear and specific (not just "Name" but context if needed)
- Smart defaults and auto-fill supported
- Progress indication for multi-step forms
- Inline validation with helpful error messages
- No CAPTCHA unless spam is a proven problem
- Mobile-optimized input types (tel, email, date pickers)

**Common failures:**
- Asking for unnecessary information upfront (company size, budget, "how did you hear about us?")
- Requiring both email AND phone on first contact
- Dropdown menus where text input would be faster
- No inline validation — errors only after submission
- Tiny tap targets on mobile
- Required fields not marked, or everything marked required

**Field count benchmarks (lead gen):**
- 1-3 fields: low friction, highest conversion
- 4-5 fields: moderate friction, acceptable for qualified leads
- 6+ fields: high friction, significant conversion drop unless value exchange justifies it

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Minimal fields, excellent UX, mobile-optimized. 7-8 = Reasonable fields, minor UX issues. 5-6 = Some unnecessary fields or UX friction. 3-4 = Too many fields or poor form UX. 1-2 = Form is broken, hidden, or absurdly long.

---

## 4. Trust Signals (Weight: High)

**What to evaluate:** Does the page give visitors reasons to trust this business?

**Trust signal hierarchy (strongest → weakest):**
1. Third-party reviews with platform logos (Google, Yelp, TripAdvisor, G2)
2. Customer testimonials with names, photos, specifics
3. Client logos or "as seen in" media logos
4. Industry certifications, licenses, awards
5. Social proof numbers ("500+ students trained", "Serving Kingscliff since 2018")
6. Guarantee or risk reversal ("100% satisfaction guarantee", "Free cancellation")
7. Security badges (SSL, payment security — mainly for e-commerce)
8. Professional photos of real team/location (not stock)

**Good:**
- 2-3 strong trust signals visible above or near the fold
- Testimonials have enough detail to be believable (not just "Great service! — John")
- Platform review scores displayed with link to verify
- Trust signals placed strategically near the CTA (reduce anxiety at decision point)

**Common failures:**
- No trust signals anywhere on the page
- Only generic stock photography — looks like every other business
- Testimonials without names, faces, or specifics (feels fake)
- Trust signals buried at the bottom where most visitors never scroll
- Outdated certifications or awards from 5+ years ago

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Multiple strong trust signals, strategically placed. 7-8 = Good trust signals but could be better positioned. 5-6 = Some trust signals but weak or poorly placed. 3-4 = Minimal trust signals, mostly generic. 1-2 = No trust signals at all.

## 5. Social Proof (Weight: Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Is there evidence that other people have chosen and benefited from this service?

**Good:**
- Specific testimonials describing outcomes ("Booked 40% more classes after working with them")
- Review counts and ratings prominently displayed
- User-generated content (photos from real customers)
- Case studies or before/after results
- Live social proof ("12 people booked this week" — if real)

**Common failures:**
- Vague testimonials ("They were really helpful!" — no specifics)
- No social proof at all on a service page
- Fake-looking testimonials (stock photos, no surnames, perfect writing)
- Social proof from irrelevant industries or audiences

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Specific, believable, outcome-focused social proof. 7-8 = Good but could be more specific. 5-6 = Present but generic. 3-4 = Weak or potentially fake-looking. 1-2 = None.

---

## 6. Urgency & Scarcity (Weight: Low-Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Is there a reason to act NOW rather than later?

**Good (when authentic):**
- Real deadlines ("Early bird pricing ends April 15")
- Genuine scarcity ("Only 8 spots per retreat")
- Seasonal relevance ("Book your summer sessions now")
- Time-sensitive incentives ("Free consultation this week only")

**Common failures:**
- Fake urgency (countdown timers that reset)
- Manufactured scarcity on unlimited digital products
- No urgency at all on pages where it's natural (events, limited capacity)
- Urgency language without specific details ("Act now!" — why?)

**Note:** Not every page needs urgency. Lead gen pages for ongoing services often don't. Booking/event pages almost always benefit from it. Score based on whether urgency is appropriate AND executed well.

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Authentic, specific urgency that drives action. 7-8 = Good urgency, slightly generic. 5-6 = Weak or missing where appropriate. 3-4 = Fake-feeling or misplaced. 1-2 = Counterproductive (damages trust) or completely missing where critical.

---

## 7. Above-the-Fold Content (Weight: High)

**What to evaluate:** What does the visitor see on their screen before scrolling? This is the most critical real estate on the page.

**Good above-fold includes (mobile priority):**
- Clear headline communicating the value proposition
- Subheadline or 1-sentence supporting detail
- Primary CTA button
- At least one trust signal or social proof element
- Relevant hero image or visual (not stock)

**Common failures:**
- Large hero image/video that pushes everything below the fold on mobile
- Navigation takes up too much vertical space
- Headline + image present but CTA requires scrolling
- Cookie consent banners, pop-ups, or chat widgets covering key content
- Slider/carousel that auto-rotates (splits attention, slows decisions)

**Mobile above-fold test:** On a standard mobile viewport (375×667px), what's visible? If the CTA isn't visible, that's a CRITICAL finding.

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Value prop, CTA, and trust signal all visible. 7-8 = Most elements present, minor issue. 5-6 = Key element missing (usually CTA or trust signal). 3-4 = Only headline visible, must scroll for everything else. 1-2 = Above fold is wasted on image/video/logo with no value communication.

---

## 8. Distraction Ratio (Weight: Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Number of things a visitor CAN do vs the ONE thing you WANT them to do.

**Ideal ratio:** 1:1 — every clickable element leads toward the conversion goal.

**Good:**
- No main site navigation (or minimal, collapsed)
- No social media links pulling visitors away
- No sidebar with unrelated content
- Footer is minimal (privacy policy, contact — not a full sitemap)
- External links open in new tab if present at all

**Common failures:**
- Full website navigation on a dedicated landing page
- Social media icons in prominent positions
- Blog posts, related articles, or "explore more" sections
- Multiple offers competing for attention
- Exit links that take visitors away from the conversion path

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Focused, single-path page. 7-8 = Mostly focused, minor distractions. 5-6 = Several unnecessary navigation elements. 3-4 = Multiple competing paths. 1-2 = Full website experience disguised as a landing page.

---

## 9. Page Goal Clarity (Weight: Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Is it immediately obvious what this page wants the visitor to do?

**Good:**
- Single, clear conversion goal (fill form, book appointment, call)
- Page structure funnels toward that goal
- Every section supports the conversion argument
- Visitor never has to wonder "what am I supposed to do here?"

**Common failures:**
- Multiple equal-weight goals (call us, fill form, download guide, visit our other page)
- Information overload that delays decision-making
- Missing connection between content sections and the conversion action
- Page reads like a brochure, not a conversion tool

**Scoring:** 9-10 = One unmistakable goal, every element supports it. 7-8 = Clear goal, minor competing elements. 5-6 = Goal present but not dominant. 3-4 = Multiple unclear goals. 1-2 = No discernible conversion goal.

---

## 10. Content Rhythm & Scannability (Weight: Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Does the page maintain visual rhythm — alternating between text, images, and interactive elements — or does it read like a wall of text? Long pages can convert well IF they're scannable. The problem isn't length — it's monotony and density.

**What good rhythm looks like:**
- Text block (2-3 short paragraphs max) → image/visual → short text → CTA → social proof → repeat
- Every 1-2 scroll heights has a "visual anchor" — an image, colored band, testimonial card, icon grid, or CTA strip
- Sections are visually distinct from each other (background color changes, dividers, spacing shifts)
- Bullet points and icon cards replace dense paragraphs wherever possible
- Headers are scannable — a visitor skimming only headers gets the full argument

**Key metrics to assess:**
- **Consecutive text blocks without visual break:** 4+ paragraphs in a row without an image, divider, CTA, or visual element = text wall
- **Words before first CTA:** Ad visitors decide in 5-8 seconds. If the first actionable element is 800+ words down, most are gone
- **Scroll depth vs content variety:** A 10x-scroll page needs 8-10 visual breaks minimum. A 3x-scroll page needs 2-3
- **Section layout variety:** Does the layout change between sections (text left/image right → full-width band → cards grid), or is every section the same template?
- **Text-to-image ratio:** Zero images is almost always a conversion problem. Sections should alternate between text-heavy and visual-heavy

**Common failures:**
- 4+ consecutive paragraphs without any visual break (text wall)
- Entire sections that are just body text — no images, icons, or visual elements
- Every section uses the same layout template (monotone scrolling)
- Dense paragraphs where bullet points or icon cards would be more scannable
- Images clustered together (gallery at bottom) instead of distributed throughout the page
- No visual anchors — scroll position 500px looks identical to 3000px
- Key content buried in long paragraphs instead of pulled out as callouts or highlighted cards

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Page breathes — text, visuals, and CTAs alternate naturally, every scroll-height has variety. 7-8 = Good rhythm with minor monotone stretches. 5-6 = Some visual breaks but long text-heavy sections. 3-4 = Mostly text walls with occasional images. 1-2 = Pure text dump with no visual rhythm.
