# Persuasion & Copy Checklist

Audit criteria for landing page copywriting and persuasion strategy. Evaluates whether the words on the page do the job of converting a visitor into a lead or booking.

---

## 1. Message-Market Match (Weight: High)

**What to evaluate:** Does the page speak to the specific audience's situation, or could it be for anyone?

**Good:**
- Copy addresses the visitor's specific problem or desire (not a generic industry overview)
- Language matches how the target audience talks (not corporate speak for a wellness retreat, not casual for B2B IT)
- Geographic and situational specificity ("yoga teacher training in Pondicherry" not "yoga teacher training")
- The first sentence on the page makes the visitor feel "this is for me"

**Common failures:**
- Generic copy that could apply to any business in the category
- Wrong tone for the audience (formal when casual is appropriate, or vice versa)
- No geographic or demographic specificity
- Copy written for the business owner, not the customer
- Industry jargon the target audience doesn't use

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Visitor immediately feels "this was written for me." 7-8 = Good fit, minor generic spots. 5-6 = Partially targeted. 3-4 = Mostly generic. 1-2 = Complete mismatch between copy and audience.

---

## 2. Benefit vs Feature Framing (Weight: High)

**What to evaluate:** Does the copy lead with what the customer gets, or just list what the service includes?

**Benefits framework:**
- Feature: what it IS → "200-hour YTT curriculum"
- Advantage: what it DOES → "Covers anatomy, philosophy, and teaching methodology"
- Benefit: what the customer GETS → "Graduate feeling confident to teach your first class"

**Good:**
- Headlines and subheads lead with benefits
- Features are present but framed through customer outcomes
- Each feature connects to a "so that you can..." statement
- Before/after transformation is clear (what life looks like after using the service)

**Common failures:**
- Page reads like a spec sheet — all features, no benefits
- Benefits are vague ("improve your life")
- No transformation narrative — just a list of what's included
- Technical features without translation for the customer

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Benefits lead everywhere, features support. 7-8 = Good benefit framing, some features standalone. 5-6 = Mix of benefits and bare features. 3-4 = Mostly features. 1-2 = Pure feature dump with no benefit framing.

---

## 3. Specificity of Claims (Weight: High)

**What to evaluate:** Does the copy make specific, verifiable claims or vague promises?

**Good:**
- Specific numbers ("Trained 500+ yoga teachers since 2018")
- Specific outcomes ("Our clients see average 3.2x ROAS in the first 90 days")
- Named credentials ("Yoga Alliance RYT-200 certified")
- Concrete details that build credibility

**Common failures:**
- Vague superlatives ("the best", "world-class", "premium quality")
- Unquantified claims ("fast results", "affordable pricing")
- Empty promises without evidence ("we guarantee satisfaction")
- Buzzwords substituting for substance ("innovative", "cutting-edge", "holistic")

**Specificity ladder (weakest → strongest):**
1. "Great food" (worthless)
2. "Authentic Indian cuisine" (slightly better)
3. "Authentic South Indian cuisine with 15+ regional specialties" (specific)
4. "Authentic South Indian cuisine — 15 regional dishes from Kerala and Tamil Nadu, all made with spices imported directly from our family's farm in Munnar" (compelling)

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Every claim is specific and verifiable. 7-8 = Mostly specific, occasional vague spots. 5-6 = Mix of specific and vague. 3-4 = Mostly vague. 1-2 = All buzzwords, no substance.

---

## 4. Objection Handling (Weight: Medium-High)

**What to evaluate:** Does the page preemptively address the reasons a visitor would hesitate?

**Common objections by page type:**
- **Lead gen (services):** "How much does it cost?", "Can I trust this business?", "How long will it take?", "What if it doesn't work?"
- **Booking (events/retreats):** "Is it worth the investment?", "What if I need to cancel?", "What's included?", "Who else is attending?", "Is the location safe/accessible?"
- **Restaurant booking:** "What's the food like?", "Is it kid-friendly?", "Parking?", "Do I need to book in advance?"

**Good:**
- FAQ section addressing top 3-5 objections
- Objections handled inline (e.g., pricing section says "includes X, Y, Z — no hidden fees")
- Guarantee or risk reversal near the CTA ("Cancel free up to 48 hours before")
- Testimonials that address common objections ("I was worried about X but...")
- Pricing transparency (even if it's "starting from $X")

**Common failures:**
- No acknowledgment of potential hesitations
- FAQ section exists but answers different questions than what visitors actually worry about
- Price completely hidden (creates distrust)
- Objections handled too far down the page (visitor has already bounced)

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Top objections addressed proactively, strategically placed. 7-8 = Most objections handled, placement could improve. 5-6 = Some objections addressed, key ones missing. 3-4 = Minimal objection handling. 1-2 = No objection handling, visitors left to figure it out.

---

## 5. Headline Formulas (Weight: Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Does the headline follow proven conversion patterns?

**Effective formulas for landing pages:**
- **Outcome-first:** "[Desired outcome] without [common pain]" — "Get certified to teach yoga without quitting your job"
- **Specific result:** "[Number] [audience] have [achieved result]" — "500+ teachers trained since 2018"
- **Question:** Addresses the visitor's primary concern — "Looking for authentic Indian cuisine in Sydney CBD?"
- **How-to:** "[How to] [achieve desired outcome]" — "How to relocate your IT infrastructure in 30 days"
- **Direct value prop:** States what you get — "200-Hour Yoga Teacher Training in Pondicherry"

**Common failures:**
- Headline is just the business name
- "Welcome to [Business]" — wastes the most important real estate
- Tries to be clever but loses clarity
- Too many ideas in one headline

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Headline follows proven formula, specific, compelling. 7-8 = Good but could be sharper. 5-6 = Functional but forgettable. 3-4 = Weak or confusing. 1-2 = Missing, irrelevant, or actively harmful.

---

## 6. Subhead & Supporting Copy (Weight: Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Does the subheadline expand on the headline and build toward the CTA?

**Good:**
- Subhead adds specificity that headline lacked ("200-hour Yoga Alliance certified program · Next cohort starts June 15 · Pondicherry, India")
- Supporting copy builds the case in 2-3 sentences, not 2-3 paragraphs
- Each section has a clear purpose in the persuasion sequence
- Copy gets more specific as you scroll down (overview → details → proof → action)

**Common failures:**
- Subhead just repeats the headline in different words
- Wall of text immediately after the headline
- Copy doesn't build toward conversion — reads like a blog post
- Sections feel disconnected (no narrative thread)

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Subhead perfectly complements headline, copy builds persuasion. 7-8 = Good support, minor flow issues. 5-6 = Functional but doesn't build momentum. 3-4 = Weak or redundant. 1-2 = Missing or counterproductive.

---

## 7. Microcopy (Weight: Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Small text that guides the user — button labels, form labels, error messages, placeholder text, help text near CTAs.

**Good:**
- CTA button text is specific and action-oriented ("Book Your Free Class" not "Submit")
- Form labels are clear and human ("Your email" not "Email Address *Required")
- Help text near CTA reduces anxiety ("No credit card required", "Takes 30 seconds", "We respect your privacy")
- Error messages tell you how to fix the problem, not just what went wrong
- Success messages confirm what happens next

**Common failures:**
- "Submit" as CTA text (tells the user nothing about what they're getting)
- "Please enter a valid email" without showing what's wrong
- No help text near the form (missed opportunity to reduce friction)
- Placeholder text as labels (disappears when typing)
- Confirmation message is just "Thank you" — doesn't set expectations for next steps

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Every small text element reduces friction or increases confidence. 7-8 = Good microcopy, minor gaps. 5-6 = Standard but unremarkable. 3-4 = Generic or missing in key spots. 1-2 = Microcopy actively creates confusion.

---

## 8. Emotional Triggers (Weight: Low-Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Does the copy connect emotionally, not just logically?

**Relevant emotions by page type:**
- **Wellness/retreat:** aspiration, transformation, escape, belonging
- **Restaurant:** craving, nostalgia, celebration, discovery
- **IT services:** relief, security, confidence, control
- **Events/training:** achievement, community, growth, fear of missing out

**Good:**
- Copy paints a picture of the after-state (not just features)
- Sensory language where appropriate (food, travel, wellness)
- Story elements that create connection
- Emotional triggers aligned with the audience and offering

**Common failures:**
- Purely logical/transactional copy (reads like a specification)
- Emotional manipulation that feels forced or insincere
- Wrong emotional tone for the audience
- Over-the-top emotional language that undermines credibility

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Emotional connection natural and aligned with audience. 7-8 = Good emotional elements. 5-6 = Present but underdeveloped. 3-4 = Flat or mismatched. 1-2 = No emotional connection or actively off-putting.

---

## 9. Reading Level Appropriateness (Weight: Low)

**What to evaluate:** Is the copy written at a level that matches the audience?

**Benchmarks:**
- Consumer services (restaurants, wellness): Grade 6-8 reading level
- Professional services (B2B, IT): Grade 8-10 reading level
- Technical audiences: Grade 10-12 acceptable if jargon is industry-standard
- General rule: simpler is better. Short sentences, short paragraphs, common words.

**Good:**
- Sentences average under 20 words
- Paragraphs are 2-3 sentences max on mobile
- Technical terms explained or avoided
- Active voice predominates

**Common failures:**
- Dense academic-style paragraphs on a consumer page
- Run-on sentences that lose the reader
- Passive voice creating distance ("Services are provided by..." instead of "We provide...")
- Unnecessarily complex vocabulary

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Perfect for the audience, effortless to read. 7-8 = Good, minor complexity issues. 5-6 = Readable but could be simpler. 3-4 = Too complex for the audience. 1-2 = Completely wrong reading level.

---

## 10. Copy Density & Sectional Flow (Weight: Medium)

**What to evaluate:** Is the copy structured in digestible sections with visual breaks, or is it dense blocks of text that overwhelm the reader? Even great copy fails if it's presented as a wall.

**Good:**
- Each section has a clear purpose and stays focused (one idea per section)
- Paragraphs are short — 2-3 sentences max, especially on mobile
- Key points pulled out as bold callouts, highlighted cards, or icon + text pairings instead of buried in paragraphs
- Sections feel complete but not exhaustive — enough to persuade, not a Wikipedia article
- Copy earns its scroll — every paragraph moves the reader closer to conversion

**Density red flags (measurable):**
- **More than 150 words in a single unbroken text block** (no subhead, image, or visual break) — too dense for ad traffic
- **Same information repeated** in different sections — redundancy inflates length without adding persuasion
- **Educational content where persuasive content should be** — event pages explaining the history of the event for 500 words instead of selling the experience
- **"About us" or origin story exceeding 100 words** — visitors care about outcomes, not backstory (backstory belongs on a separate page)
- **No hierarchy within sections** — a section that's all body text with no bold, no callouts, no structure is a skim-killer

**The sectional test:** Remove all images and visuals from the page mentally. Does the text alone have rhythm? Short paragraph → subhead → bullet list → short paragraph → callout → CTA. Or is it paragraph → paragraph → paragraph → paragraph?

**Common failures:**
- 500+ word sections with no subheads or visual structure within them
- Repeating the same value proposition 3 different ways across the page
- History/background sections that are longer than the "why attend" or "what you get" sections
- Body copy that reads like a blog post — informative but not structured for conversion
- No bold text, callouts, or visual hierarchy within text-heavy sections — everything has the same visual weight

**Scoring:** 9-10 = Every section earns its space, copy is sectioned with rhythm and hierarchy, zero redundancy. 7-8 = Good structure, minor dense spots. 5-6 = Some sections well-structured but others are text-heavy. 3-4 = Multiple text walls, significant redundancy. 1-2 = Entire page reads like a document, not a conversion page.
