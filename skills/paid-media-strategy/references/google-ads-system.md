\# Google Ads Expert Reference Guide 2025–2026

\#\# 1\. Campaign Types and Objectives

\#\#\# Core campaign types as of 2025–2026

Google Ads exposes multiple campaign types: Search, Display, Video, Shopping, App, Performance Max, Demand Gen (which replaced Discovery), and Local/Local Services in a separate system. Performance Max (PMax) is a goal‑based type that can serve across Search, Shopping, YouTube, Display, Discover, Gmail, and Maps from a single campaign. Demand Gen campaigns focus on visual, mid‑funnel reach across YouTube (including Shorts), Discover, Gmail, and now Google Display inventory. Standard Shopping campaigns remain available alongside Shopping‑enabled PMax, with Smart Shopping fully replaced by PMax by 2023–2024.\[1\]\[2\]\[3\]\[4\]\[5\]\[6\]\[7\]\[8\]

\#\#\# Search campaigns

Search campaigns serve text ads on Google Search and search partners based on keyword or dynamic matching. Variants include standard keyword‑based search, Dynamic Search Ads (DSA) that match queries to site content, and brand vs non‑brand structures.\[9\]\[3\]

\*\*Bidding strategies typically available\*\*

\- Manual CPC and Enhanced CPC (ECPC).\[10\]\[11\]  
\- Maximize Clicks (traffic focus).\[11\]  
\- Maximize Conversions and Maximize Conversion Value (with optional tCPA/tROAS fields that behave like Target CPA/Target ROAS).\[12\]\[11\]  
\- Target CPA, Target ROAS (as explicit strategies or as targets on the Maximize strategies).\[13\]\[12\]  
\- Target Impression Share for Search visibility.\[11\]

\*\*Targeting options\*\*

\- Keywords (broad, phrase, exact).\[14\]\[9\]  
\- Dynamic Search Ads: URL rules and page feeds instead of keywords.  
\- Audiences: in‑market, affinity, detailed demographics, custom segments, remarketing, Customer Match applied at campaign/ad group level in observation or targeting modes.\[15\]  
\- Location, language, device, ad schedule, and demographic filters.\[16\]

\*\*Ad format requirements\*\*

Search uses Responsive Search Ads (RSAs) by default; each RSA allows up to 15 headlines and 4 descriptions with 30/90 character limits respectively, plus 2 path fields at 15 characters each.\[17\]\[18\]

\*\*Practical minimum budgets\*\*

Officially there is no hard minimum, but Smart Bidding needs 30–50 conversions per month per campaign to stabilize, which usually implies budgets large enough to generate that volume in your vertical. For lead gen with moderate CPCs, many practitioners target at least the equivalent of 50–100 clicks per ad group per month before making aggressive decisions.\[19\]\[20\]

\#\#\# Performance Max (PMax)

PMax is a goal‑based, fully automated campaign that reaches across all Google channels, designed to complement Search campaigns rather than replace them. It optimizes in real time for conversions or conversion value using Smart Bidding and Google AI, taking inputs such as asset groups, audience signals, feeds, and conversion value rules.\[5\]\[21\]\[22\]

\*\*Supported bidding\*\*

\- Maximize Conversions with optional target CPA.  
\- Maximize Conversion Value with optional target ROAS. Manual bidding is not supported.\[23\]\[21\]

\*\*Targeting and control\*\*

\- No direct keyword or audience targeting; Google decides targeting based on goals and signals.\[24\]\[5\]  
\- Audience signals and search themes act as hints, not hard filters; PMax can show to audiences outside those signals if it predicts conversions.\[25\]\[24\]

\*\*Minimum budgets\*\*

Google does not publish explicit minimums, but Smart Bidding guidance still applies: target at least 15–30 conversions in 30 days, with 30–50 per month preferred for stable learning.\[26\]\[13\]\[19\]

\#\#\# Display campaigns

Display campaigns show responsive display creatives across the Google Display Network (GDN) and some Gmail/YouTube partner placements. They support:\[6\]

\- Manual CPC / ECPC, Maximize Clicks, Maximize Conversions, Target CPA, Target ROAS depending on subtype.\[10\]\[11\]  
\- Targeting by audiences (in‑market, affinity, custom, remarketing, Customer Match), topics, placements, and content keywords.\[27\]\[15\]  
\- Responsive Display Ads with up to 5 headlines (30 chars), 1 long headline (90 chars), and up to 5 descriptions (90 chars), plus up to 15 images and 5 logos.\[28\]

Display is typically used for remarketing, upper‑funnel awareness, or supporting Demand Gen with cheaper reach. For Smart Bidding, aim for at least 30–50 conversions per month per campaign; otherwise consider manual or Max Clicks for learning.\[20\]\[19\]

\#\#\# Video campaigns (YouTube)

Video campaigns support several formats:\[29\]

\- Skippable in‑stream ads (CPV or tCPA/tROAS/Maximize Conv bidding) that can be skipped after 5 seconds and can run before, during, or after videos and on video partners.\[30\]\[31\]  
\- Non‑skippable in‑stream ads up to 15–20 seconds in many markets (billed on CPM and focused on reach).\[31\]  
\- In‑feed video ads (formerly video discovery) that appear in YouTube search, related videos, and home feed.\[29\]  
\- Bumper ads: 6‑second non‑skippable CPM units for quick awareness.\[31\]  
\- YouTube Shorts ads optimized for vertical video under 60 seconds in a Shorts‑only placement.\[29\]

Bidding options include CPV, Target CPM, Target CPA, Maximize Conversions/Conversion Value and occasionally Target Impression Share depending on subtype. Targeting is primarily audience‑based (in‑market, affinity, custom, remarketing) and contextual (topics, placements, keywords).\[27\]\[15\]\[11\]\[29\]

\#\#\# Demand Gen campaigns

Demand Gen campaigns replaced Discovery campaigns starting 2024 and run across YouTube (including Shorts, in‑feed, in‑stream), Discover, Gmail, and as of 2025 Google Display inventory. They are designed for visually rich, mid‑funnel engagement and demand creation rather than pure direct response.\[32\]\[2\]\[33\]\[1\]

\*\*Bidding and objectives\*\*

\- Supports conversions, clicks, and engagement objectives, with Smart Bidding options like Maximize Conversions, Maximize Conversion Value, tCPA, and tROAS for performance‑oriented setups.\[1\]

\*\*Targeting\*\*

\- Manual audience targeting (unlike PMax) using in‑market, affinity, custom segments, remarketing, Customer Match and lookalike segments (lookalikes are explicitly supported in Demand Gen).\[15\]\[1\]

\*\*Creative requirements\*\*

\- Single image, video, and carousel formats with strict specs: up to 20 images per ad in multiple aspect ratios (1.91:1, 1:1, 4:5, 9:16) with minimum and recommended resolutions and 5MB max size.\[34\]\[35\]  
\- Carousels support 2–10 cards with consistent aspect ratios and similar resolution requirements.\[36\]\[35\]

Demand Gen is preferred when the goal is to warm defined audiences with high‑impact visuals and you need more placement and reporting control than PMax, especially for YouTube‑heavy creative testing.\[37\]\[38\]

\#\#\# Shopping campaigns

Shopping campaigns require a linked and approved Google Merchant Center (now largely Merchant Center Next) account with a compliant product feed. Two main options exist:\[39\]\[40\]

\- Standard Shopping: ads on Search and Shopping tab with manual bid and structure control, generally using manual CPC, ECPC, or Maximize Clicks/Convs and optional tROAS.\[7\]\[41\]  
\- Performance Max with Merchant Center feed: Shopping served via PMax with automated bidding, cross‑channel reach, and lower control but higher scale.\[8\]\[7\]

Standard Shopping is preferred when granular product‑level control, query visibility, and defensive bidding are critical; Shopping‑enabled PMax is better when scale and automation outweigh transparency.

\#\#\# App campaigns

App campaigns (formerly Universal App Campaigns) promote app installs or in‑app actions across Search, Play Store, YouTube, and Display. They use Smart Bidding only (e.g., tCPA or tROAS based on install or in‑app conversion value) and auto‑generate ads from text, images, and store assets.\[6\]

\- App install objective: bids toward cost per install or per first open.  
\- App engagement objective: optimized for specific in‑app events.

\#\#\# Local and Local Services campaigns

Legacy Local campaigns were migrated into PMax for store goals; local inventory ads now run via Shopping/PMax with Merchant Center local feeds. Local Services Ads (LSAs) run in a separate interface, appearing at the very top of local SERPs with a pay‑per‑lead model and Google Guarantee/Screened badging.\[42\]\[43\]\[44\]\[6\]

LSAs take service categories, service areas, and business verification as inputs and charge per valid lead (call/message), with bidding options like Maximize leads, target cost per lead, or manual max per‑lead bids. Targeting is largely automated and based on service type plus geography rather than traditional keywords.\[44\]\[45\]\[46\]

\#\#\# Minimum budgets by campaign type (practical)

No hard platform minimums exist, but Smart Bidding effectiveness depends on conversion volume; official tables for Display suggest campaigns with fewer than 30 conversions see high CPA/ROAS volatility and up to 4‑week learning periods versus 2 weeks when there are ≥100 conversions. Multiple independent analyses and Google guidance point to 30–50 conversions per month per campaign as the baseline for stable Smart Bidding. For manual/Max Clicks campaigns without Smart Bidding, practitioners often target at least 300 clicks per month per campaign to obtain statistically meaningful data before major changes.\[47\]\[48\]\[19\]\[26\]\[20\]

\#\# 2\. Bidding Strategies and Decision Logic

\#\#\# Available bidding strategies

Google Ads supports manual and automated strategies:\[49\]\[11\]

\- Manual CPC.  
\- Enhanced CPC (ECPC) overlaying manual bids.\[49\]\[10\]  
\- Maximize Clicks.  
\- Maximize Conversions.  
\- Maximize Conversion Value.  
\- Target CPA.  
\- Target ROAS.  
\- Target Impression Share.  
\- For video: CPV (Cost‑per‑View), vCPM (viewable CPM), CPM.\[49\]\[31\]

Automated strategies that optimize for conversions/value (Max Conversions/Value, tCPA, tROAS) are collectively Smart Bidding.\[16\]

\#\#\# Maximize Conversions

Maximize Conversions is a fully automated bid strategy that sets bids to get the highest possible number of conversions within the daily budget, ignoring individual CPC caps. You can optionally add a target CPA, which makes the strategy behave like Target CPA while retaining Maximize Conversions labeling.\[12\]\[11\]\[49\]

\*\*When to use\*\*

\- New campaigns/accounts needing data where CPA is important but you can tolerate volatility.  
\- When you have at least 15–30 conversions per month and a flexible CPA target.

\*\*Risks\*\*

\- Can aggressively spend full budget on low‑value conversions if conversion value varies widely.\[11\]  
\- If used with a too‑low target CPA, it can throttle volume or get stuck in learning.

\#\#\# Target CPA (tCPA)

Target CPA automatically sets bids to try to get as many conversions as possible at or below a defined cost per acquisition on average, optimizing at auction time using many contextual signals. Historically Google recommended at least 15 conversions in the last 30 days for Target ROAS and roughly similar or higher thresholds for tCPA; independent practitioners now suggest 30 conversions per month per campaign as a practical minimum for stable performance.\[50\]\[51\]\[52\]\[13\]\[12\]

\*\*How to set targets\*\*

Google recommends starting near your recent historical CPA (e.g., within ±20%) and adjusting gradually. If historical CPA is 50, a starting tCPA between 50–60 is safer than 30; once stable performance is reached, lower the target in small steps, giving at least 1–2 conversion cycles (often weeks) between changes.\[13\]\[26\]\[16\]

\#\#\# Maximize Conversion Value

Maximize Conversion Value bids to capture the highest total conversion value within budget, without a specific ROAS constraint. It is most useful for e‑commerce or lead gen where each conversion has a meaningful value, either passed via tags or configured through value rules.\[53\]\[54\]\[11\]

Adding an optional target ROAS converts the strategy into Target ROAS behavior while keeping the Maximize Conversion Value label.\[12\]\[11\]

\#\#\# Target ROAS (tROAS)

Target ROAS optimizes for conversion value per cost, trying to achieve an average return on ad spend equal to the target. Google’s official guidance notes that most campaign types require at least 15 conversions with valid conversion values in the past 30 days to use tROAS effectively; more volume (e.g., 50+ conversions/month) produces more stable performance.\[51\]\[13\]

\*\*Target selection\*\*

\- Calculate historical Conv. value / cost, multiply by 100 to get an initial target ROAS % (for example 4.0 value/cost → 400% tROAS).\[13\]  
\- Start with a target at or below historical ROAS, then raise it gradually as performance stabilizes.\[13\]

\#\#\# Maximize Clicks

Maximize Clicks automatically sets bids to get as many clicks as possible within budget and is suitable for traffic generation, early keyword research, brand awareness when conversions are not yet tracked, or in very low‑volume B2B niches. It can be constrained by a max CPC cap at campaign or portfolio level to avoid outlier CPCs.\[10\]\[11\]

\#\#\# Manual CPC and Enhanced CPC

Manual CPC allows explicit per‑keyword or ad group bids, giving maximum control but no automatic optimization. Enhanced CPC (ECPC) is a semi‑automated overlay that adjusts manual bids up or down for clicks more or less likely to convert, using Smart Bidding signals while honoring the base bid structure.\[55\]\[10\]

Manual CPC still makes sense when:

\- You need strict bid caps (e.g., in highly competitive legal verticals).  
\- Conversion tracking is unreliable or unavailable.  
\- You are collecting data prior to moving into Smart Bidding.

\#\#\# Target Impression Share

Target Impression Share sets bids with the goal of showing your ads on the absolute top, top, or anywhere on the first page of Search results for a given share of eligible impressions. It is most appropriate for brand defense (owning your branded queries) or critical generic terms where share of voice is a key KPI.\[56\]\[11\]

\#\#\# CPV and CPM bidding for video

Skippable in‑stream ads can use CPV bidding where you pay per view (30 seconds or full ad), while non‑skippable and bumper ads usually use target CPM (tCPM) or vCPM for reach/frequency objectives. For awareness campaigns, CPM bidding with Reach or Video Reach campaigns is standard; performance‑oriented video campaigns increasingly use Maximize Conversions or tCPA based on video‑view‑assisted conversion behavior.\[30\]\[31\]\[29\]

\#\#\# Portfolio bid strategies

Portfolio bid strategies allow the same Smart Bidding strategy (e.g., tCPA or tROAS) to be shared across multiple campaigns, ad groups, or keywords, pooling conversion data. This is useful when individual campaigns lack volume but the aggregate has sufficient conversions (e.g., 50+ per month) to train a robust model.\[16\]\[11\]

\#\#\# Smart Bidding signals

Smart Bidding uses a wide range of auction‑time signals, including device, physical location, location intent, weekday/time of day, browser, operating system, and more, to predict conversion probability and value. These signals, combined with account history, drive automated bid adjustments at each auction instead of relying on static bid modifiers.\[57\]\[50\]

\#\#\# Minimum conversion volume and transitions

While Google has removed many hard thresholds, official and ecosystem guidance converge on:

\- Fewer than 30 conversions in a period → high volatility and long learning (up to 4 weeks).\[19\]\[20\]  
\- Around 50 conversions → medium volatility and 2–3 week learning.\[26\]\[20\]

A common progression:

1\. Launch with Manual CPC or Maximize Clicks to build click and search‑term data (aim for 300+ clicks per month).\[48\]  
2\. Switch to Maximize Conversions once you are tracking conversions reliably and have at least 15–30 per month.\[19\]  
3\. Once stable CPAs/ROAS and adequate volume (30–50+ conversions/month) are present, move to tCPA or tROAS, starting targets close to recent performance and adjusting slowly.\[51\]\[13\]

\#\# 3\. Keyword Strategy and Match Types

\#\#\# Match type behavior in 2025–2026

Google supports three positive match types: broad, phrase, and exact. Broad match now uses machine learning to match to queries related to the user’s intent, taking into account landing pages, other keywords, and signals; its behavior is significantly more expansive than phrase or exact. Phrase match has become looser over time, often behaving closer to older broad match by matching to queries that include the meaning of the keyword in more flexible ways. Exact match is no longer “exact string only”; it includes close variants that Google considers to have the same meaning or intent, including reordered words and some synonyms.\[58\]\[59\]\[14\]\[9\]

Google itself recommends broad match combined with Smart Bidding for most Search campaigns to maximize reach and performance within goals, and phrase/exact when stricter control is required (e.g., sensitive verticals).\[9\]

\#\#\# Negative keyword match types and account‑level negatives

Negative keywords can be applied at account, campaign, and ad group levels, with match types (broad, phrase, exact) controlling how strictly they block queries. Account‑level negative keywords, introduced globally around 2023, allow a single list of up to 1,000 terms to exclude across all Search and Shopping inventory (including the Search/Shopping portion of PMax). Negative match types differ from positive ones: negative broad requires all words to be present in any order, phrase blocks queries containing the exact phrase, and exact blocks only that exact query.\[60\]\[61\]\[62\]

\#\#\# Keyword research and intent mapping

Modern keyword research focuses on mapping queries to commercial intent stages rather than building exhaustive SKAG‑style lists. Best practices include:

\- Segmenting by intent: informational ("what is"), commercial investigation ("best", "reviews"), transactional ("buy", "near me"), and branded vs non‑branded.\[63\]\[14\]  
\- Using tools plus the Search terms report to iteratively refine coverage and negatives, particularly as broad/phrase match can pull in many variants.\[62\]\[58\]

\#\#\# Search terms report and n‑gram analysis

The Search terms report shows queries that triggered your ads and generated impressions/clicks/conversions; this is crucial for promoting high‑value queries to keywords and adding negatives for poor performers. N‑gram analysis (grouping terms by recurring words/phrases) is widely used by practitioners to identify systematically wasteful patterns (e.g., "cheap", "free", "jobs") and winning qualifiers to expand on, especially in broad match campaigns.\[62\]\[58\]\[9\]

\#\#\# Quality Score and keyword quality

Quality Score is a 1–10 diagnostic metric based on expected CTR, ad relevance, and landing page experience. Improving Quality Score typically involves:\[64\]\[65\]

\- Increasing ad relevance by aligning RSA assets closely with query themes and ad group keywords.  
\- Improving CTR with strong offers and extensions/assets.  
\- Optimizing landing pages for relevance, speed, and mobile UX.

Raising Quality Score generally reduces CPCs at a given position and improves impression share, although Smart Bidding somewhat obscures the direct effect.\[65\]

\#\#\# Keyword cannibalization

With overlapping match types and broad/phrase behavior, multiple ad groups or campaigns can compete for the same queries, causing cannibalization. PMax can also serve on queries targeted by Search campaigns if budgets and priorities allow. Monitoring search‑term overlaps and prioritization rules is essential; Search campaigns with identical broad/phrase/exact keywords generally take precedence over PMax, but PMax can still serve branded queries if Search is budget‑limited or targeting is stricter.\[66\]\[67\]\[5\]

\#\#\# SKAGs vs modern ad groups

Single‑keyword ad groups (SKAGs) and hyper‑granular structures have fallen out of favor as match types and Smart Bidding became more intent‑based; practitioners now report that simpler, more consolidated structures perform better in 2025\. Instead of SKAGs, modern structures often group 5–20 tightly related queries per ad group, relying on RSAs, negative keywords, and audience layering to maintain relevance while giving Smart Bidding enough data.\[14\]

\#\#\# Responsive Search Ads and pinning

RSAs allow up to 15 headlines and 4 descriptions; Google tests combinations to learn which perform best. Ad strength scores rate asset diversity and relevance but are not direct ranking factors; they correlate with better performance when used as a guideline rather than a hard rule.\[18\]\[68\]

Pinning headlines/descriptions fixes them to specific positions (e.g., pin brand to position 1, main CTA to description 1), which can improve control but may reduce ad strength and testing breadth. A common best practice is light pinning of critical compliance or brand elements while leaving other slots unpinned for optimization.\[18\]

\#\#\# Dynamic Keyword Insertion (DKI)

Dynamic keyword insertion uses syntax like \`{KeyWord:Default Text}\` in headlines or descriptions to insert the triggered keyword dynamically, improving ad relevance. It is most useful in tightly themed ad groups where all keywords are safe to show in the ad; avoid DKI with competitor names or sensitive terms to prevent policy issues.\[9\]

\#\# 4\. Audience Targeting

\#\#\# Audience segment types

Google Ads now consolidates many options under "audience segments" with major categories:\[15\]

\- In‑market segments: users with recent purchase intent in specific categories (hundreds of verticals).  
\- Affinity segments: long‑term interests and habits (150+ high‑level lifestyle categories).\[27\]  
\- Detailed demographics: long‑term life facts like education level, homeownership, marital status, employment, and parental status.\[69\]\[27\]  
\- Your data segments: website and app visitors, customer lists (Customer Match), and YouTube users (viewers, subscribers, engagers).\[15\]  
\- Lookalike segments: in 2025 these are available specifically in Demand Gen campaigns to reach people similar to a seed list.\[1\]\[15\]

Similar audiences ("similar segments") were deprecated in 2023; Google stopped generating new similar segments in May 2023 and removed them from campaigns by August 2023, replacing them with audience expansion and optimized targeting in Display/Video and Smart Bidding‑driven discovery of new users.\[70\]\[71\]\[72\]

\#\#\# Custom segments

Custom segments (replacing custom affinity and custom intent labels) allow building audiences from keywords, URLs, and apps that describe interests or purchase intent; they can be used across Search, Display, Video, Demand Gen, and Discovery‑style placements. For example, a custom segment might include competitor URLs, in‑market style keywords, and topical sites to refine mid‑funnel targeting.\[69\]\[27\]

\#\#\# Remarketing and Customer Match

Remarketing includes:

\- Website visitors (via Google tag or GTM), optionally segmented by behavior.  
\- App users based on in‑app events.\[15\]  
\- YouTube viewers (viewed, subscribed, liked, etc.).\[15\]  
\- Customer Match lists built from first‑party CRM data hashed and uploaded to Google Ads, which can be used for targeting and exclusions.\[15\]

Customer Match has specific formatting and consent requirements; Google’s enhanced conversions for leads and Consent Mode v2 changes reinforce the importance of explicit user consent for data use in EEA/UK.\[73\]\[74\]\[75\]

\#\#\# Demographics and detailed demographics

Standard demographics (age, gender, parental status, household income) can be used for bid adjustments or exclusions. Detailed demographics go deeper into education, homeownership, marital status, and employment type, allowing refined audience definitions.\[76\]\[69\]\[27\]

\#\#\# Observation vs targeting

Audience segments can be applied in two modes:

\- \*\*Targeting\*\*: restricts delivery to users in the selected audiences (ads only shown to those segments), useful for remarketing lists, Customer Match, or tightly defined in‑market segments.\[77\]\[78\]  
\- \*\*Observation\*\*: does not restrict delivery but collects performance data for segments, allowing bid adjustments and insights without limiting reach; recommended default for Search until you have strong evidence for hard targeting.\[79\]\[77\]

\#\#\# Combined audiences

Combined segments allow Boolean logic (AND/OR/NOT) across audience types, such as "in‑market for travel" AND "household income top 30%" AND NOT "recent converters". These are particularly powerful in Display/Video/Demand Gen, but must be balanced with volume.\[69\]\[15\]

\#\#\# Audience signals in PMax

Audience signals in PMax are suggestions to Google’s AI about who is likely to convert; they are not hard targeting constraints. They can include:\[25\]\[24\]

\- Your data lists (remarketing, Customer Match).  
\- In‑market and affinity segments.  
\- Custom segments.

Best practice is to include strong first‑party signals (past buyers, high‑value lead segments) plus relevant in‑market categories to accelerate PMax learning and steer it toward profitable lookalikes.\[80\]\[81\]

\#\# 5\. Performance Max Deep Dive

\#\#\# How PMax works

PMax is a goal‑based campaign that uses Smart Bidding and Google AI to allocate budget across all available channels (Search, Shopping, Display, YouTube, Discover, Gmail, Maps) based on conversion or conversion value goals. Advertisers provide:\[4\]\[5\]

\- Asset groups (text, images, video, logos) and optional Merchant Center or page feeds.  
\- Audience signals and search themes.  
\- Conversion goals and values (including value rules and new vs existing customer settings).\[5\]\[53\]

Google’s auction‑time bidding and optimized targeting then decide which combinations and placements to show, using conversion tracking and bidding goals as the core steering inputs.\[24\]\[50\]

\#\#\# Controllable vs uncontrollable elements

\*\*You control:\*\*

\- Budget and bid strategy (Max Conversions/Value with or without targets).\[21\]  
\- Asset groups and creative assets; which feeds/URLs are in scope.  
\- Audience signals and search themes.  
\- Brand exclusions and negative keywords (as of 2025, including campaign‑level negatives).\[82\]\[5\]

\*\*You do not control directly:\*\*

\- Exact queries, placements, or per‑channel budget splits.  
\- Relative weighting between Search vs Shopping vs YouTube, beyond indirect influence via conversion performance.\[4\]\[24\]

\#\#\# Asset group strategy

Asset groups function similar to ad groups but operate across all channels; common segmentation:

\- By product category or collection (for Shopping feeds).\[83\]  
\- By funnel stage or audience (prospecting vs remarketing signals).\[80\]  
\- By value tiers (high‑margin vs low‑margin SKUs).\[84\]

Best practice is to avoid over‑fragmentation; most guides recommend starting with 1–3 asset groups per distinct objective or category and ensuring enough conversion volume per group.\[83\]\[4\]

\#\#\# Search themes

Search themes allow advertisers to specify up to 50 queries or phrases per asset group that customers use, acting as additional signals for PMax’s query matching across Search and Shopping. They are optional and additive, respecting brand exclusions and account‑level negatives, and have the same prioritization as phrase and broad match keywords in Search.\[85\]\[86\]\[67\]\[5\]

\#\#\# Reporting and Insights

PMax integrates with the Insights tab and provides:

\- Search term category insights (not full raw queries) and asset combination reports.\[3\]\[5\]  
\- Channel‑level performance reporting (Search, Shopping, YouTube, Display, etc.) introduced and expanded in 2025.\[2\]\[82\]

Practitioners still consider PMax relatively opaque vs standard Search/Shopping because detailed placement and search‑term‑level reporting is limited, though 2025 updates improved transparency.

\#\#\# Brand exclusions and negatives

Brand exclusions prevent PMax from serving on specified branded queries, helping avoid cannibalization of brand Search campaigns. Account‑level negative keywords also apply to the Search/Shopping portion of PMax, and as of 2025 campaign‑level negative keywords can exclude specific terms for PMax, though they only cover Search/Shopping, not Display/YouTube/Gmail.\[87\]\[85\]\[5\]

\#\#\# PMax for different business models

\- \*\*E‑commerce:\*\* Use Merchant Center feed, robust conversion value tracking, and value rules; segment asset groups by product category and margin; consider separate campaigns for new customer acquisition vs all customers.\[8\]\[4\]  
\- \*\*Lead gen:\*\* Require high‑quality conversion tracking (including offline imports and enhanced conversions) and tight lead quality feedback; consider PMax mainly once Search campaigns are mature, and ensure strong negatives and brand exclusions to avoid spam traffic.\[82\]\[24\]  
\- \*\*App:\*\* PMax can support app promotion goals but classic App campaigns are often preferred for install‑focused strategies due to more app‑specific levers.\[6\]

\#\#\# Cannibalization of Search

Data shows that in most mature accounts PMax overlaps significantly with Search campaigns: one analysis across 503 accounts found keyword overlap between PMax and Search in over 90% of accounts, impacting more than half of Search campaigns and nearly 28% of ad groups. Google’s prioritization rules state that exact, phrase, and broad match keywords in Search campaigns take precedence over PMax when the user’s query exactly matches the keyword, but PMax can still serve branded queries when Search budgets are limited or targeting is stricter.\[67\]\[66\]\[5\]

Mitigation tactics include:

\- Strong brand Search campaigns with adequate budget and possibly higher priority bidding.  
\- PMax brand exclusions and account‑level negatives for key branded queries.  
\- Separate PMax campaigns for discovery vs Shopping‑centric goals.

\#\#\# When not to use PMax

Avoid or delay PMax when:

\- Conversion tracking is weak or untrustworthy; PMax will optimize toward whatever it can measure, often low‑quality signals.\[24\]  
\- You need fine control over queries/placements (e.g., in regulated industries or strict B2B niches).  
\- You are pre‑product market fit and need learnings from granular testing more than automated scale.

\#\#\# URL expansion and Final URL behavior

Final URL expansion allows PMax to dynamically replace your specified final URL with more relevant URLs from your domain based on user intent, creating dynamic headlines and descriptions. It is on by default; if you disable it, PMax will only send traffic to URLs in your asset groups and page feeds.\[88\]\[89\]

URL expansion is powerful for large e‑commerce catalogs but risky for tightly curated funnels or compliance‑sensitive content. Best practices:

\- For e‑commerce with broad catalogs, leave URL expansion on but use page feeds and URL exclusions to prioritize and protect specific sections.\[89\]\[88\]  
\- For lead gen or single‑offer funnels, often disable URL expansion and explicitly restrict URLs to avoid sending traffic to generic pages or unrelated content.\[90\]\[91\]

\#\# 6\. Conversion Tracking and Measurement

\#\#\# Google tag (gtag.js)

The Google tag (gtag.js) is the unified tag for Google Ads and Analytics; it loads the tag library and configures measurement IDs, with conversion event snippets implemented as \`gtag('event','conversion', { ... })\` calls specifying \`send\_to\`, \`value\`, and \`currency\` parameters. Best practices include placing the base tag in the \`\<head\>\` and event snippets on conversion pages or firing them via GTM.\[92\]\[93\]\[94\]

\#\#\# Google Tag Manager (GTM)

GTM wraps tags, triggers, and variables in a container, allowing no‑code deployment of the Google tag, conversion tags, enhanced conversions, and other vendors. Key elements:\[95\]\[92\]

\- Triggers (page view, click, form submission, custom events).  
\- Variables (GA4/Ads configuration, dataLayer values, CSS selectors, user‑provided data objects).  
\- Conversion Linker to ensure proper Google Ads click ID (GCLID) handling.\[95\]

\#\#\# Enhanced conversions

Enhanced conversions for web send hashed first‑party customer data (email, phone, name, address) along with conversion events to improve match rates and modeled conversions across devices and browsers. Setup involves enabling Enhanced Conversions in Google Ads under the conversion action’s settings and implementing user‑provided data via Google tag or GTM (automatic, manual CSS selectors, or code configuration). For leads, "enhanced conversions for leads" extends offline import workflows with user‑provided data for better matching and bidding.\[96\]\[97\]\[75\]\[95\]

\#\#\# Consent Mode v2

Google’s EU Consent Mode v2 became mandatory in March 2024 for continued use of personalized advertising and measurement features for EEA/UK users. Without Consent Mode v2 integrated via a Google‑certified CMP, Google Ads and GA4 will stop collecting data for new EEA users for ad personalization and may disable remarketing and conversion tracking features.\[98\]\[74\]\[73\]

Consent Mode v2 introduces four consent parameters: \`analytics\_storage\`, \`ad\_storage\`, \`ad\_user\_data\`, and \`ad\_personalization\`, which inform Google tags how to behave when consent is denied, enabling modeled conversions where allowed.\[99\]\[98\]

\#\#\# Offline conversion import and GCLID

Offline conversion import connects ad clicks to offline outcomes via the Google Click ID (GCLID) appended to URLs with auto‑tagging. The typical flow is click → GCLID captured on lead form → stored in CRM → offline conversion recorded (e.g., closed deal) → conversion with GCLID uploaded back to Google Ads via CSV or API. Enhanced conversions for leads can supplement or partially replace GCLID with user‑provided data when click IDs are missing.\[100\]\[101\]\[75\]\[102\]

\#\#\# GA4 integration vs native conversions

Google Ads can import GA4 events as conversions or use native Ads conversion tags; GA4 provides richer cross‑channel reporting but adds complexity and potential delays, while Ads tags provide more direct, low‑latency feedback into Smart Bidding. Many practitioners now favor native Ads tags (with GA4 also running for analytics) for primary bidding signals.\[92\]

\#\#\# Attribution models

Google Ads offers Last click and Data‑driven attribution (DDA); DDA is now the default for most conversion actions and uses account‑specific data to assign fractional credit across touchpoints. Last click remains available but is no longer recommended except in special cases (e.g., simple funnels, low volume) because it ignores assist interactions.\[103\]\[104\]\[105\]

\#\#\# Conversion counting, windows, and view‑throughs

Conversion actions can be set to count "one" (e.g., leads) or "every" (e.g., e‑commerce transactions) per ad interaction and can use configurable conversion windows (e.g., 30, 60, 90 days) and view‑through conversion tracking for Display/Video. Lead gen accounts should be explicit about which events truly represent business value and avoid over‑counting micro‑conversions.\[75\]\[92\]

\#\#\# Conversion value rules

Conversion value rules adjust conversion values based on audience, location, and device to better reflect LTV or margin differences; they can add or multiply value by factor and apply at account or campaign level. Examples include boosting value for certain geos (e.g., multiplying value by 2 for US or by 4 for target states) or for new vs existing customers.\[106\]\[107\]\[54\]\[53\]

\#\#\# Server‑side tagging

Server‑side GTM and similar setups proxy tags via first‑party subdomains, improving data resilience against browser restrictions while still needing proper consent and privacy controls. It is especially useful for high‑value e‑commerce and B2B sites where data quality is critical.\[98\]\[95\]

\#\#\# iOS and privacy impacts

App Tracking Transparency (ATT) on iOS and broader privacy changes reduce deterministic signal availability; Google relies more on modeled conversions and aggregated reporting. Enhanced Conversions and Consent Mode v2 mitigate these impacts by leveraging hashed first‑party data and consented modeling, but practitioners should expect more conversion lag and heavier reliance on modeled data, especially for iOS and Safari traffic.\[97\]\[98\]

\#\# 7\. Ad Formats and Creative Best Practices

\#\#\# Responsive Search Ads (RSAs)

Specifications:

\- Up to 15 headlines (30 characters each) and 4 descriptions (90 characters each).\[17\]\[18\]  
\- 2 optional path fields of up to 15 characters each.\[18\]

Best practices:

\- Provide at least 8–10 distinct headlines, mixing core keywords, UVPs, objections, and CTAs.  
\- Avoid over‑pinning; pin legally required or critical brand messages only.  
\- Use ad strength as a directional measure but prioritize performance tests over blindly chasing "Excellent".\[18\]

\#\#\# Responsive Display Ads (RDAs)

RDAs support up to 5 headlines (30 chars), 1 long headline (90 chars), 5 descriptions (90 chars), 15 images, and 5 logos. Use high‑contrast images with minimal text, clear focal points, and consistent branding.\[28\]

\#\#\# Video ad formats

Key specs:\[31\]\[29\]

\- Skippable in‑stream: skippable after 5 seconds, recommended lengths 15–20 seconds for awareness and 2–3 minutes for deeper consideration, billed on CPV or tCPA/Max Conv.\[30\]  
\- Non‑skippable in‑stream: up to 15 seconds in many markets, CPM bidding.\[31\]  
\- Bumper: 6 seconds, non‑skippable CPM.  
\- In‑feed: thumbnail plus text that looks like organic videos in search/home/related feeds.  
\- YouTube Shorts: vertical, \<60 seconds, optimized for mobile Shorts placement.\[29\]

Best practices include front‑loading the hook in the first 3–5 seconds, clear branding early, and strong visual cues to support sound‑off viewing.

\#\#\# Demand Gen creative specs

Demand Gen supports images, videos, and carousel ads with specific asset specs:\[35\]\[34\]

\- Image creatives: up to 20 images per ad; recommended 1200×628 (1.91:1), 1200×1200 (1:1), 960×1200 (4:5), 1080×1920 (9:16), each ≤5MB.  
\- Carousels: 2–10 images with consistent aspect ratio across cards.

Google recommends uploading at least 3 images per aspect ratio to maximize placement coverage.\[35\]

\#\#\# Shopping ads and feeds

Shopping ads are generated automatically from Merchant Center feeds, using attributes like title, description, product type, Google product category, price, and custom labels. Product titles are the single most impactful attribute; they can be up to 150 characters, but only \~70 show prominently on many devices.\[108\]\[84\]

Feed optimization best practices include:

\- Title formulas: Brand \+ Product Type \+ Key Attributes (size, color, material) \+ variant details.\[84\]\[108\]  
\- Using custom labels (0–4) to tag products by margin, season, price tier, or promo status for bid rules.\[109\]\[110\]  
\- High‑quality images without overlays and accurate pricing/availability.

\#\#\# Assets (extensions)

Google now refers to extensions as "assets"; major types include sitelinks, callouts, structured snippets, image, call, lead form, location, affiliate location, price, promotion, and app assets.\[111\]\[112\]

Examples:

\- Sitelinks: additional deep links with up to 25‑character descriptions per line.\[112\]\[113\]  
\- Callouts: 25‑character snippets for USPs like "Free Shipping".\[114\]  
\- Structured snippets: header plus up to 10 values (25 chars each) listing product types, amenities, etc.\[114\]  
\- Price assets: up to 8 items with price and URL.\[111\]  
\- Promotion assets: time‑bound offers with promo code or discount details.\[112\]

Assets increase CTR and can improve Quality Score; use all relevant assets and monitor asset‑level performance.

\#\#\# Ad customizers

Ad customizers support dynamic insertion of countdown timers, locations, and keyword‑dependent values to increase relevance and urgency. They are particularly useful for:\[9\]

\- Promotion countdowns in RSA descriptions.  
\- Location‑insertion for multi‑location businesses.  
\- Device‑specific messaging.

\#\#\# Ad strength indicator and auto‑applied recommendations

Ad strength evaluates the diversity, relevance, and quality of RSA assets; it correlates with performance but should not override data‑driven testing.\[18\]

Auto‑applied recommendations can change bids, budgets, match types, and ads based on Google’s optimization score engine; practitioners recommend tightly controlling which auto‑applies are enabled and being especially wary of broad match expansion and aggressive budget increase recommendations that primarily drive spend.\[115\]\[116\]\[117\]

\#\# 8\. Campaign Structure and Account Architecture

\#\#\# Consolidated vs granular structures

Overly granular structures (SKAGs, many small campaigns) fragment data and slow Smart Bidding; newer guidance encourages consolidated campaigns with fewer, larger ad groups built around coherent themes. Consolidation improves conversion volume per campaign/ad group, speeding learning and improving bid model stability.\[20\]\[14\]\[83\]\[19\]

Granularity is still warranted where:

\- Business goals differ materially (e.g., different ROAS targets, budgets by geo).  
\- Product lines have distinct economics or seasonality.

\#\#\# Segmentation logic

Common segmentation axes:

\- Brand vs non‑brand Search; brand often uses separate campaigns with lower CPA targets and Target Impression Share.  
\- Funnel stage: generic prospecting vs category vs product terms.  
\- Geography: separate campaigns for distinct countries or regions when economics differ.  
\- Product/service category for Shopping and PMax (by feed labels).

\#\#\# Naming conventions and labels

Consistent naming and labels support reporting and automation (scripts and SA360). Typical patterns encode objective, network, geo, funnel stage, and bidding strategy (e.g., \`SEA | US | Brand | Exact | tCPA\`). Labels tag experiments, promos, and semantic groups for easier filtering and script logic.\[118\]\[65\]

\#\#\# Budget allocation and shared budgets

Budgets should align with expected conversion volume and priority:

\- Protect brand and highest‑ROAS campaigns with sufficient budget to avoid "limited by budget" status.  
\- Use shared budgets sparingly; they simplify budget management across many small campaigns but can mask under‑performance and misallocate spend.\[64\]\[115\]

\#\#\# Experiments

Google Ads Experiments allow controlled A/B testing by splitting traffic between base and trial setups for bidding, creatives, landing pages, and more. Experiments now support Search, Shopping, some PMax experiments, and video; they randomize traffic and budget, report on lift, and help determine statistical significance.\[119\]\[120\]\[121\]

Typical tests:

\- Max Conversions vs tCPA.  
\- Manual CPC vs Smart Bidding.  
\- Broad vs phrase/exact‑only.  
\- New landing page variants.

\#\#\# Scripts and automated rules

Google Ads scripts enable JavaScript automations for budget alerts, pausing low‑performers, reporting, and Quality Score tracking. Common scripts:\[118\]\[65\]

\- Budget monitors that email when daily spend exceeds a threshold.\[122\]  
\- Quality Score trackers logging scores daily to Sheets for trend analysis.\[65\]

Automated rules provide simpler logic (e.g., pause keywords with 0 conversions and CPA above a threshold after X clicks).

\#\# 9\. Google Merchant Center and Shopping

\#\#\# Merchant Center and Merchant Center Next

Merchant Center is required for Shopping ads, PMax with feeds, and free listings; Merchant Center Next, rolling out globally and replacing classic Merchant Center by 2024–2025, simplifies product management and introduces tools like Product Studio and unified online/local product views.\[123\]\[124\]\[39\]

Key changes in Merchant Center Next include automatic data ingestion from websites, a revamped interface, and centralized insights and diagnostics.\[39\]

\#\#\# Feed requirements and optimization

Feeds must include required attributes (id, title, description, link, image\_link, price, availability, condition, GTIN/brand as applicable) and pass policy and data quality checks. Optimization focuses on titles, product types, Google product categories, and precise attribute usage.\[40\]\[110\]\[108\]\[84\]

\- Titles: up to 150 characters; front‑load brand \+ product type \+ key attributes and keywords.\[108\]\[84\]  
\- Descriptions: expand on attributes not obvious from title/image, supporting long‑tail queries.\[84\]  
\- Custom labels: used for bidding segmentation by margin, season, promo; up to 5 labels per product.\[110\]\[109\]

\#\#\# Supplemental feeds and feed rules

Supplemental feeds and feed rules let you enrich primary feeds (e.g., adding custom labels or overriding titles) without changing the source system. Rules can extract attributes, prepend brand, or categorize products programmatically.\[110\]\[108\]

\#\#\# Diagnostics

Merchant Center diagnostics show item‑level disapprovals, warnings, and opportunities; fixing feed errors and policy violations is critical for Shopping/PMax coverage.\[39\]

\#\#\# Free listings vs paid Shopping

Free listings display products from Merchant Center organically on the Shopping tab, Search, Images, Discover, and sometimes Gmail/YouTube, without ad spend. Shopping ads share the same feed but are served via Google Ads campaigns with CPC/Smart Bidding.\[125\]\[126\]

Paid Shopping ads get priority placements and more volume, while free listings are governed by feed quality and price competitiveness rather than bids.\[127\]\[128\]

\#\# 10\. Reporting, Optimization & Scaling

\#\#\#Key metrics for decision‑making  
Search prominence

Search impression share: impressions / eligible impressions; low share with good CPA usually signals under‑bidding or under‑budgeting.

Search top impression share and absolute top impression share: share of impressions in top positions and very first ad position; use alongside CPC/CPA to decide where to push or back off.

\#\#\#Efficiency & quality

Quality Score (QS): still a diagnostic (1–10) based on expected CTR, ad relevance, and LP experience; improving QS generally lowers CPC at a given position.

Conversion metrics: CVR, CPA, ROAS, cost per conversion value—always read in context of attribution model and lag.

For PMax/Demand Gen, give more weight to conversion value / cost and new‑customer value than vanity metrics (clicks/views).

\#\#\#Optimization Score & Recommendations  
Optimization score: 0–100% estimate of how well account/campaign is “set up to perform” based on Google’s best‑practice models, not an ROI metric.

Google claims that accounts increasing optimization score by 10 points via recommendations see a median 14% conversion lift, but that’s across a broad sample and highly goal‑dependent.

How to use it like a pro

Treat it as a triage tool:

Fix structural issues: missing conversions, disapproved ads, missed critical extensions/assets.

Be cautious with recs that:

Force broad match expansion everywhere.

Aggressively raise budgets without clear incremental profit.

Auto‑apply creatives you can’t QA.

Many advanced buyers deliberately sit at 70–85% score after dismissing misaligned recs, not 100%.

Budget‑constrained campaigns  
Signals a campaign is genuinely budget‑limited, not just “wants more”:

“Limited by budget” status plus:

High impression share loss (budget) but acceptable CPA/ROAS.

Search IS (rank) reasonably strong at current bids.

Actions:

First, prioritize:

Shift budget from worse CPA / lower ROAS campaigns to constrained winners.

Second, scale:

Raise daily budget in 20–30% steps, monitor CPA/ROAS and impression share.

If using tROAS/tCPA, consider slightly relaxing target before further budget increases to avoid throttling.

When to kill keywords / ad groups / campaigns  
No official rules, but commonly used thresholds:

Keywords (non‑brand Search)

100–200 clicks, 0 conversions, while other terms in same campaign are converting → candidate for pause or bid cut, especially in lead gen.

If CPA is 2×–3× target after a few conversions and no clear explanation (e.g., new LP test), consider pausing or pushing to exact only.

RSAs & assets

After a few thousand impressions per asset, pause assets consistently under‑performing vs peers in the same RSA (where the interface surfaces asset strength/performance) while maintaining at least 5–8 “best” assets per ad.

Campaigns

If a Smart‑Bidding campaign has had 50+ conversions and 2–3 full conversion windows and still can’t approach target CPA/ROAS, re‑evaluate:

Conversion definition & quality (are you counting junk?).

Structure (over‑segmented, starving data).

Landing page/offer mismatch.  
Killing the campaign is a last resort after these are addressed.

Scaling approaches that actually work  
Budget & target tuning

For tROAS: lower ROAS target to trade margin for volume; ensure you know minimum viable ROAS given your LTV/margin.

For tCPA: raise target CPA gradually to let system find more volume, then optimize creative/LP to bring CPA back down.

Inventory expansion

Match types: move from exact/phrase‑only to include broad in dedicated campaigns once tracking is strong.

Geos: expand from core markets to similar performance tiers (e.g., same language, comparable CPCs).

New surfaces:

Add Demand Gen for visual mid‑funnel.

Add PMax once Search/Shopping are efficient, not before.

Creative expansion

Rotate new RSA assets monthly; keep a “hall of fame” of top‑performing headlines/descriptions as a base set.

For video/Demand Gen: test new thumb‑stopping hooks and offers rather than micro‑tweaks to bids.

Structural adjustment

Consolidate fragmented campaigns so each Smart‑Bidding entity has 30–50+ conversions/month.

Split only where economics differ (geo, margin bands, funnel stage).

Seasonality & Smart Bidding  
Smart Bidding already models “normal” seasonality, but you can override short, exceptional events via Seasonality Adjustments at bid‑strategy level (Search & Display).

Recommended for:

1–7 day events with expected big CVR changes (Black Friday, Diwali, major promos).

Implementation:

Tools & Settings → Bid Strategies → Advanced controls → Seasonality adjustments.

Enter date range and expected CVR uplift/drop.

Don’t use for long (\>14 days) periods; let the model learn organically instead.

Auction Insights & competitive analysis  
Auction Insights shows: impression share, overlap rate, position‑above rate, top‑of‑page rate, absolute‑top‑of‑page rate, and outranking share vs other advertisers in the same auctions.

How senior buyers use it:

Identify real auction competitors (not just people you think are competing).

Track trends:

If your impression share drops while competitor share rises, consider a competitive response (higher bids, better qs/creatives, or focusing on less‑contested segments).

Protect brand:

If competitors aggressively appear on your brand, combine Target Impression Share on brand Search with brand‑specific messaging, and consider legal action if trademark misuse occurs.

11\. Recent Changes & Emerging Features (2024–2026)  
Use this section as “delta” vs older best practices.

Performance Max evolutions  
Search Themes:

Introduced to give more control over which queries PMax can pursue; behave like hints with similar priority to phrase/broad keywords.

Brand Exclusions:

Now standard; let you exclude your own or other brands from PMax search coverage, reducing cannibalization and unwanted competitor bidding.

Negative Keywords for PMax:

Initially only at account level; by 2025 you can add PMax campaign‑level negatives affecting the Search/Shopping portion.

Reporting:

Better breakdowns by channel (Search vs Shopping vs YouTube vs Display) and more insights in PMax reports and Insights tab from 2025 onward.

Demand Gen evolution  
Full replacement of Discovery:

Discovery campaigns migrated to Demand Gen; new features like carousels, Shorts‑first video, and expanded Display supply.

Channel controls:

Ability (rolling out) to decide which surfaces Demand Gen uses (e.g., YouTube only, Shorts focus, etc.).

Lookalike segments:

Demand Gen includes explicit lookalike audiences based on your data segments, making it closer to Meta‑style prospecting.

AI‑generated assets & creative  
Google is steadily adding AI‑assisted asset creation (headlines, descriptions, image suggestions, and even AI‑generated Demand Gen assets) inside Ads UI.

Practitioner stance:

Useful for ideation and filling “quantity” requirements.

Needs strict brand/offer QA; many advanced teams disable auto‑applied creative recommendations and only use AI suggestions manually.

Match type & query behavior updates  
Tightening of search query reporting (less raw SQR visibility), but incremental improvements post‑2023 giving slightly more queries back.

Official line continues to push broad match \+ Smart Bidding as the default; expect further erosion of behavioral differences between phrase and broad over time.

Consent Mode v2 enforcement  
From March 2024, failing to implement Consent Mode v2 with a certified CMP for EEA users leads to:

Loss or degradation of conversion tracking and remarketing capabilities for new EEA users.

Limited access to personalized ads.

This makes first‑party data and compliant consent flows non‑negotiable for serious advertisers in affected regions.

Deprecations & removals  
Similar audiences/segments:

Deprecated in 2023; all removed by Aug 2023; rely on lookalikes (where available), optimized targeting, and Smart Bidding expansion instead.

Broad Match Modifier:

Already deprecated pre‑2023; legacy BMM was effectively rolled into updated phrase match.

Standalone Discovery campaigns:

Migrated into Demand Gen during 2024\.

Google AI Overviews / SGE impact  
AI Overviews / SGE in organic results:

Still evolving, but implications:

Some informational queries show fewer traditional organic/paid listing folds above the fold.

Commercial queries tend to maintain or increase ad prominence around AI modules, suggesting ad load will stay strong but SERP patterns will keep shifting.

For you, this mostly means:

Watch conversion rates and CPCs around launch waves.

Double down on intent‑driven keyword segmentation and creative that addresses AI‑generated answer content (e.g., “Still comparing X tools? Here’s why Y wins…”).

YouTube Shorts & new video surfaces  
Shorts ads:

Now a core part of YouTube inventory with Demand Gen and Video campaigns using vertical assets; purchase and lead‑gen use cases are growing.

Best practice:

Treat Shorts like TikTok/Reels: fast hook, native style, vertical, strong on‑screen text; don’t recycle 16:9 TV spots and expect performance.
